## Introduction

Ever since the early days of neural networks, there has been a dream: **building an AI system as intelligent as a human**. This dream of artificial general intelligence (AGI) remains one of the most inspiring goals in AI.

But the path to get there is not clear and could be very difficult. Will we see breakthroughs within our lifetimes, or might it take centuries or even longer?

## ANI vs AGI: Understanding the Difference

<Note>
There's been a lot of unnecessary hype about AGI, partly because AI actually includes two very different things.
</Note>

### ANI: Artificial Narrow Intelligence

**ANI** (Artificial Narrow Intelligence) is an AI system that does **one thing**, a narrow task, sometimes really well and incredibly valuable:

- Smart speakers
- Self-driving cars
- Web search
- AI in specific applications (farming, factories)
- Image recognition
- Speech recognition

<Note>
Over the last several years, ANI has made **tremendous progress** and is creating tremendous value in the world today.
</Note>

### AGI: Artificial General Intelligence

**AGI** (Artificial General Intelligence) is different - it's the hope of building AI systems that **could do anything a typical human can do**.

### The Confusion

Because ANI is a subset of AI:
- Rapid progress in ANI → Tremendous progress in AI (true)
- Some people conclude: Progress in AI → Progress toward AGI (questionable)

<Warning>
Despite all the progress in ANI and therefore tremendous progress in AI, it's unclear how much progress (if any) we're really making toward AGI.
</Warning>

## Why Simulating the Brain Is Hard

With the rise of modern deep learning, there was hope that simulating lots of neurons would lead to human-level intelligence. Unfortunately, it's not that simple.

### Two Major Challenges

<Steps>
  <Step title="Artificial Neurons Are Too Simple">
    A logistic regression unit is **nothing like** what a biological neuron does. Our artificial neurons are vastly simpler than real neurons in your brain.
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="We Don't Understand the Brain">
    Even to this day, we have **almost no idea how the brain works**. There are still fundamental questions about how neurons map inputs to outputs that we don't understand.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Note>
Given our very limited understanding of how the human brain works, trying to simulate it as a path to AGI will be an incredibly difficult path.
</Note>

## A Ray of Hope: The One Learning Algorithm Hypothesis

Despite the challenges, there's fascinating evidence that keeps the AGI dream alive.

### Remarkable Brain Plasticity

Fascinating experiments on animals show that **the same piece of brain tissue can do a surprisingly wide range of tasks**.

This has led to the **One Learning Algorithm Hypothesis**: Maybe a lot of intelligence could be due to one (or a small handful) of learning algorithms.

<Note>
If we could figure out what that algorithm is, we might be able to implement it in a computer someday.
</Note>

## Evidence: Brain Rewiring Experiments

These experiments demonstrate the brain's remarkable adaptability:

### Auditory Cortex Learns to See

**Experiment by Roe et al.:**

1. The **auditory cortex** normally processes sound from your ears
2. Researchers cut the connection between ears and auditory cortex
3. Instead, they fed **images** to the auditory cortex
4. Result: **The auditory cortex learned to see!**

<Note>
A piece of brain designed for hearing learned to process vision when given visual data.
</Note>

### Somatosensory Cortex Learns to See

**Similar experiment:**

1. The **somatosensory cortex** normally processes touch
2. Researchers rewired it to receive visual input
3. Result: **The somatosensory cortex learned to see!**

### Pattern

Many different parts of the brain, just depending on what data is given, can learn to:
- See
- Feel  
- Hear

As if there's **one algorithm** that, depending on input data, learns to process that input accordingly.

## Human Sensory Adaptability

Fascinating systems have been built showing human adaptability:

### Seeing with Your Tongue

**System:**
- Camera mounted on forehead
- Maps images to voltage patterns on the tongue
- **Result**: People can learn to "see" with their tongue

### Human Echolocation

**Experiment:**
- Train humans to make clicking sounds
- Listen to how sounds bounce off surroundings
- **Result**: Humans can learn some degree of sonar (like bats and dolphins)

### Haptic Belt for Direction Sense

**Device:**
- Ring of buzzers around waist
- Programmed with magnetic compass
- North-facing buzzer vibrates
- **Result**: People develop a direction sense - they just "know" where north is

### Third Eye on Frog

**Experiment:**
- Surgically implant third eye on frog
- **Result**: The brain just learns to use this new input

<Note>
These experiments show that the brain is **amazingly adaptable** (neuroscientists say "plastic") to a bewildering range of sensor inputs.
</Note>

## The Key Question

If the same piece of brain tissue can learn to:
- See
- Hear
- Feel
- Process other sensory inputs

**The question is**: What is the algorithm the brain uses, and can we replicate it in a computer?
